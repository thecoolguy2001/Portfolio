July 2025

Visual Design Direction

AR filters live in a vertical phone viewport with the user's own face in frame. The visual language had to read in three seconds — TikTok scroll velocity is the design constraint.

Face is the canvas

Filter elements anchor to facial landmarks. UI sits in the safe corners of the frame, never blocking the player's face.

Score that reads on mute

Viewers scroll TikTok with sound off. Score updates animate big and bold so the game reads silently.

Color = bonus

Saturated, distinct hues for power-ups so they pop in any background. Neutral elements stay grayscale to keep visual hierarchy clean.

Built to record

Every filter is designed for the post-game share. End screens hold long enough to capture, and personal best stays visible during the recording window.

June 2025

Target Users

Two user types drove the design — the active creator looking for content fuel, and the casual scroller who tries a filter once. The filters had to work for both.

CC

The Casual Creator

Posts a few TikToks a week, looking for content fuel

Goals

  • Filter that becomes a video by itself
  • Reaction-driven content with scoring
  • Easy to record, easy to caption

Pain Points

  • Most filters are visual only — no payoff to record
  • Game filters often cover the face entirely
SS

The Social Scroller

Tries filters they see, sends to friends

Goals

  • Quick gratification, low investment
  • Score to compare with the friend who sent it
  • One-tap to challenge a friend back

Pain Points

  • Most filters don't work as a back-and-forth
  • Hard to tell which filters are games at all

June 2025

Problem

Most TikTok filters are either purely visual or static. Users engage with them briefly but rarely return. There's a missed opportunity to turn filters into repeatable micro games.

The challenge was creating AR experiences that users would want to play multiple times and share with friends.

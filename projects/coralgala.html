May 2026

Production Push — Bug Fixes & Final Polish

Six months of monthly upgrades have brought Coral Gala close to where it always wanted to be. The current focus is the final pre-production work: rounding off bugs surfaced during the 3D rebuild, tightening edge-case behavior, and polishing the small inconsistencies that don't show up in feature lists but do in everyday use. The goal is a real production release — not another experimental drop.

Earlier versions — the August 2025 beta, the October 2025 v1 — were never positioned as production builds. They were attempts to make a fun, weird, shared application for friends and family — low-stakes ways to test whether a real-time persistent tank was worth building seriously. The signal those early versions sent back was strong enough to justify the year of 3D work that followed, and now the project is finally being prepared for an actual launch.

From friends-and-family to production

Beta and v1 were built for a small audience — share the link, see what happens, iterate on what they said. Production is built for everyone else — and the bar shifted accordingly.

Bug list before features

The current cycle is explicitly closed to new features. Until the existing 3D system is stable end-to-end, every improvement is a fix or a polish — no scope creep before the launch.

The signal that justified the rebuild

The friends-and-family versions were rough on purpose. They proved the premise — calm, shared, persistent, weird in a good way — and that proof is what made the 3D investment defensible.

May 2026

Realistic Coral & Positioning

The latest pass on the 3D environment: real coral models, properly placed. Coral now sits where it would in a real reef — anchored to rock structures, at the right scale, with directional placement that reads as deliberate rather than scattered. The tank starts to read as a place, not a void with fish in it.

April 2026

Realistic Lighting & Shading on the 3D Environment

Underwater lighting applied across the whole 3D environment. Light rays diffuse the way they would beneath the surface, depth fades the further-back coral, and fish pick up directional shading as they cross light pools. Shading isn't a polish layer — it's what makes the tank feel like water rather than a black box with fish in it.

March 2026

Realistic Feed Animation

Feeding now reads correctly. Pellets fall through the water column with proper drift, fish notice and converge with intent, and once eaten the pellet disappears with a small motion cue. Feeding is the most-used interaction in the tank — it had to feel good, not just functional.

February 2026

Realistic Fall Into Water

When a fish or pellet enters the tank, it now behaves like it's actually entering water — splash on impact, slowed descent, surface ripple radiating outward. A small detail with a massive feel difference. The tank stops reading as a render and starts reading as an aquarium.

January 2026

Fish Swimming Animation Improvement

The first big upgrade after the 3D rebuild: real swimming. Fish bodies bend along their length, tails counter-rotate against direction changes, and acceleration / deceleration follow water-resistance physics. The earlier rigid-body fish read as 3D models gliding through space — now they read as alive.

November 2025

3D Version Begins

After the v1 ship in October, the obvious next move was 3D. The 2D experience had carried the project as far as it could — to feel alive, the tank needed depth, real water behavior, and three-dimensional fish moving through volume rather than across a plane. November was the start of that rebuild, with each following month dedicated to a specific layer of realism.

Realism is layered, not all at once

The 3D rebuild was scoped as a series of monthly improvements rather than one giant ship. Swimming, fall-into-water, feed, coral, lighting — each got its own pass instead of being bundled into one fragile release.

3D doesn't mean louder

The original goal — calm, ambient, "weirdly peaceful" — had to survive the move to 3D. Every realism improvement got reviewed against that bar. If it pulled the tank away from feeling restful, it didn't ship.

October 2025

v1 Released — Water Effects & Fish Models

The first proper version of Coral Gala shipped in October 2025. The cone-fish placeholders from the beta were replaced with real fish models, and the canvas got proper water effects — caustic shimmer, surface motion, ambient currents. Persistent state via Firebase meant your fish stayed when you closed the tab, and a friend's fish would still be swimming when you came back.

This was the milestone where the project stopped being a sketch and started being a real, sharable thing. It also exposed the ceiling of the 2D approach — within a month, planning had begun for the 3D rebuild.

Real fish models

The cones from beta were retired. v1 shipped with proper fish models, sized and colored across a small starter set.

Water effects

Caustic shimmer, surface motion, gentle ambient currents. The canvas finally read as water, not as a flat background.

Persistent state

Firebase wired in so fish, settings, and tank state survived refresh. Multiple users saw the same shared tank — the social premise was real for the first time.

August 2025

Beta — 2D Experience with Cone Fish Models

The first playable build was a beta in August: a flat 2D canvas with cone-shaped placeholder fish gliding around. No water effects, no real models, no persistence — just the core idea on a screen, proving the loop. Crude on purpose. The point of the beta was to validate the feeling of a calm, shared, persistent tank before investing in the visual layer that would carry it.

Cones, not fish

Placeholder geometry on purpose. Spending time on art assets before the interaction loop was right would have been working backwards.

2D first to test the feeling

If the experience didn't feel calm and inviting in 2D, it wouldn't in 3D either. The beta was the cheapest way to find out whether the premise held.

What the beta proved

People stayed in the tank longer than expected, came back, and shared the link. Enough signal to commit to v1 — fish models, water effects, persistence — in October.

July 2025

Visual Design Direction

The aesthetic had to read as calm at every step. People come to an aquarium to slow down — the visual choices were all in service of that.

Soft palette, no neon

Ocean blues and warm corals at low saturation. Bright accents sit only on the fish themselves, never on the chrome.

Slow motion as default

Fish drift, the camera doesn't. Nothing in the UI moves faster than the fish — the tank dictates the tempo.

Minimal HUD

Controls live in a single corner panel that hides on idle. The full surface belongs to the tank.

Type as a whisper

Light-weight sans, low contrast text. Reading copy is never the goal — looking at fish is.

June 2025

Target Users

Two visitor types shaped the experience. Both wanted something low-pressure on a tab — but for different reasons.

IT

The Idle Tabber

Keeps a relaxing tab open while doing other work

Goals

  • Calm visuals to glance at between tasks
  • Nothing demanding attention or sound
  • A tab worth leaving pinned for weeks

Pain Points

  • Most ambient sites autoplay loud audio
  • Static screensavers feel dead, not alive
CR

The Casual Returner

Comes back to check on "their" fish

Goals

  • A small sense of ownership without an account
  • See that the world has changed since last visit
  • Share the tank link with a friend

Pain Points

  • Most sites lose state when you close the tab
  • Multiplayer toys usually require sign-up

June 2025

The Problem

Despite the popularity of ambient apps and screensavers, most virtual fish tanks are static, single-user, or tied to outdated software.

There's a gap in fun, lightweight web-based experiences that users can visit casually—but return to often.

June 2025

Project Started

Coral Gala is a virtual fish tank experience built as a live, browser-based aquarium that users can interact with in real time.

Designed with playful animation, atmospheric sounds, and multiplayer persistence—serving as both a digital toy and a social experiment.

May 2026

Finalizing Issue No. 1

The first physical issue of The Pulp is in final design. Cover art, section dividers, story typesetting, and the back-cover reader-submission card are all in their last passes. The goal is a magazine that earns the same long-form attention that drew me to print in the first place — something a reader holds, finishes, and waits for the next one of.

Print-first layout

Designed for paper from the start: real grid, real typography, real bleed and trim. Not a website screenshot pasted into InDesign.

News + pulp, side by side

The original dual-content idea finally has the right format — short, edited news pieces in the front; long-form pulp fiction in the back; a center spread for art.

Reader submission card

A physical card bound into each issue that readers tear out, write a story on, and mail back. The accepted ones run in the next issue.

Collectible by design

Numbered issues, consistent spine treatment, and a visual identity that rewards lining them up on a shelf. Each issue should make the reader want the next one.

October 2025

Renamed: Old York Times → The Pulp

The old name was a play on The New York Times — clever, but it told you about the reference, not about the product. Once the project was about a physical magazine that lives or dies by its emotional pull, the name had to do that work too. The Pulp is shorter, more honest about what it is, and gives the magazine its own identity instead of borrowing one.

From reference to identity

"Old York Times" pointed at another publication. "The Pulp" points at itself — the paper, the genre, the feeling of holding it.

One-word names print better

A masthead, a spine, a cover stamp, a tear-out card all benefit from a name that fits cleanly. Two syllables, hard consonants, easy to set in any size.

Emotional, not academic

The product is asking readers to commit time and shelf space. The name should feel like the thing on the shelf, not like a study of it.

September 2025

Pivot to Print

The whole concept started as: what would news and shared stories look like without modern social media in the way? Physical print does that naturally — newspapers, magazines, and pulp paperbacks pull readers deeper because there's nothing competing for the next swipe. After living with the website build, it was clear the web version kept fighting that idea instead of expressing it. So the project pivoted to what it had always been trying to be: a real, physical magazine.

Format matched intent

The goal was the depth physical print creates between reader and content. A printed magazine reaches that on the first page; a website kept asking the user to opt back in to it every scroll.

Collect, don't refresh

Print rewards collecting. Issues stack, build a back-catalog, and live on a shelf. That's the relationship the project wanted with its readers — not session metrics.

The website becomes the shop

The web presence shifts to supporting the magazine — issue archive, subscription, story submission portal — instead of trying to be the publication itself.

August 2025

Pre-launch Reassessment

The site was close to launch and I'd been using it daily, with a small group of testers doing the same. The honest read from both ends was the same: as a product, it wasn't strong enough to ship. The aesthetic worked. The reading experience worked. What was missing was a reason to come back — the depth and ritual that print delivers without effort, and that the web kept undercutting.

What testers said

"Looks beautiful, read one story, didn't come back." Repeated almost word-for-word across separate testers. Aesthetic alone wasn't holding them.

What I felt using it

The site was competing with my phone's home screen. Even with the period chrome, the medium kept signaling "scroll past me."

What was working

Typography, column structure, edition cadence, writer voice. None of those needed to be thrown away — they just needed the right format to live in.

The decision

Don't ship a weak product to meet a self-imposed launch date. Pull the plug on launch and put the work into the medium that actually serves the original idea.

June 2025

Reader Story Submission

Pulp magazines historically ran reader-submitted stories — a real two-way relationship with the audience instead of a broadcast. I started building a submission flow into the site so any reader could send in a short story, with the editor picking the best for the next edition. It was the right idea; in hindsight it was a sign the project wanted to be a real magazine, not a digital approximation of one.

Submission form

Title, byline, body, optional author note. Word-count guidance matched the article template so submissions came in roughly print-ready.

Editorial queue

Backend view to read, accept, request edits, or decline. Accepted pieces flowed into the next edition's layout draft.

Reader credit

Bylines for accepted authors, with a short author bio. The submission was always meant to feel like real publication, not a guestbook.

May 2025

Bringing in Real Writers

The AI-generated pulp content was replaced with stories from actual writers. The difference was immediate — voice, character, pacing, restraint. The page templates I'd built for "stories" finally had stories on them. This was the moment the project started to feel like a real publication, even if it was still living on the wrong medium.

Recruiting

Writer outreach

Reached out to short-fiction writers from lit-mags and Substack circles. Pitched the project's tone, paid per piece, gave them the column/drop-cap template up front so they wrote into the format.

Editorial

Light edit, strong frame

The design system did the period work; writers wrote in their own voice. No house-style enforcement beyond word counts and copy edits.

Outcome

Real stories changed the design

Real writing exposed places where the template was over-styled. Drop-caps got smaller, pull-quotes became opt-in, gutters tightened. The design improved because the content got better.

April 2025

Companion App Begins

Started a companion app for the original Old York Times release — a phone-shaped reading experience that mirrored the website's editions in a more intimate, print-like format. The app got far enough to validate the reading flow on a small screen before the larger pivot to print made it a candidate for repurposing rather than shipping as-is.

Edition-based, not feed-based

Like the site, the app showed one edition at a time. No infinite scroll, no algorithmic timeline — open the app, read this issue, close it.

Native typography

Same display serifs, same column logic, scaled and re-grided for phone. The reading experience was meant to feel like the broadsheet folded in half twice — not a different product.

Now: the magazine companion

After the print pivot, the app's natural role is companion to the magazine — issue archive, audio reads, submission portal — rather than a standalone publication.

March 2025

The AI Story Problem

To get the site populated, the first wave of pulp stories was generated with AI. It looked like content. It wasn't. The stories had structure but no voice — the thing that makes pulp pulp. I knew it was a weak approach the moment it shipped, and the only honest fix was to bring in real writers. The site can carry vintage typography and column rules forever; without a writer behind a story, none of it lands.

Form needs voice

Drop caps and column rules are stage dressing. Without a real writer, the page is a stage with no actor on it.

Pulp = pulpy

The genre lives on personality, oddness, restraint, and surprise. AI-generated text averaged all of those into nothing.

Decision: pay people

The cost of real writers is the cost of a real publication. Anything cheaper than that wasn't actually the project I was trying to make.

February 2025

Visual Design Direction

Old York Times had to evoke 1930s newspaper without becoming a costume. The visual language pulls genuine details — column grid, hairline rules, drop caps — and pairs them with modern reading affordances.

Period typography

Display serifs for headlines, transitional serifs for body. Italic small caps for bylines. The type does most of the period-setting work.

Grid like a press sheet

Multi-column layout with narrow gutters and hairline rules. Mimics letterpress structure without sacrificing screen readability.

Cream paper, ink black

Off-white background reduces glare and reads as paper. Pure black for text — period-correct and high contrast.

Modern only where it helps

Search, navigation, sticky headers — modern conveniences hide inside vintage chrome so the reading experience stays in character.

August 2024

The Problem

Looking at current news and article sites, they seem to have become less expressive and creative for users, with some even adding unwanted complexity to their design.

The challenge was to craft a digital experience that captures the essence of old pulp fiction and 1930s news while meeting today's standards for usability.

July 2024

Initial Content Approach

Early on the plan was to fill news sections with curated current stories — tech, science, culture — and to fill the pulp section with short fiction generated by AI as placeholder content while the design and platform came together. The intent was always to replace placeholder pulp with real writing later; this was the scaffolding stage, not the editorial stage.

News

Sectioned, not algorithmic

Tech, science, culture, and editorial were separate sections — closer to a paper's index than a homepage feed. The reader chose what to read by topic, not by what was loudest.

Pulp

AI as placeholder

AI-generated short fiction filled the pulp section to test layout, length, and pacing. Treating it as real content turned out to be a mistake — captured later in the March 2025 entry.

Editorial Loop

Edition cadence

Issues were planned as discrete editions, not a rolling feed. The cadence forced clear handoff points between writing, art, and layout — and gave the publication a real publishing rhythm.

June 2024

Project Started — A UI/UX Concept

Old York Times began as a UI/UX concept, not a launch plan. The question was simple: what would news and shared stories look like if modern social media wasn't shaping how we read them? Physical print — newspapers, magazines, pulp paperbacks — has always pulled readers deeper, because there's nothing competing for the next swipe. The reader sits with the page, and the page lets them grow into the story or the world it's describing.

The first goal was to recreate that depth on the web — to give modern news and short fiction a layout, pace, and identity that asked for the reader's full attention. The pulp magazine style was the lens: nostalgic in form, fresh in voice, and built around the idea that content is something you spend time with, not something you skim past.

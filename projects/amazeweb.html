May 2026

Current Direction

Six years in, Amaze Web is still primarily a client services shop — but four new threads run alongside the client work as the service expands.

Framer templates for sale

After two years of building Framer client sites, the first batch of templates is being prepared for the Framer marketplace. Same craft, different distribution — for buyers who don't need a custom build.

AI agent integrations

Exploring partnerships with AI agents to plug into client e-commerce sites — automated assistants for product discovery, customer support, and online-shop maximization. Goal: clients get more out of their store without hiring more people.

Email service design

Beginning to take on email design alongside web — branded campaigns, transactional flows, and lifecycle systems for the same kinds of clients already getting their sites built here.

Marketing push

Clients have come through inquiry and word-of-mouth since 2020. The next phase is to actively market — putting the service in front of the buyers it's been quietly serving for years.

2025

Building the First Framer Templates

After two years of Framer client work, started designing templates to sell on the platform. The discipline is different — a template has to read as flexible enough for many brands without losing its own personality. Each one gets the same care a client site would, just designed for a wider audience.

2024

Framer Joins the Toolkit

Added Framer to the stack for client sites. Its blend of clean design control, native motion, and tight CMS handling fit creative-leaning clients who needed visual polish without giving up the ability to edit content themselves. Most new client sites this year shipped on Framer.

Right tool, right client

Framer doesn't replace Webflow or WordPress on the menu — it joins them. The right platform for a client depends on the client's content rhythm, technical comfort, and budget.

Motion as default, not feature

Framer makes motion cheap to design well. Clients get sites that move with intent, not just sites that scroll.

2022 – 2023

Steady Client Work

A run of client projects across small businesses, indie brands, and creators — most arriving through inquiry, referrals, and word-of-mouth. The work covered Webflow rebuilds, WordPress refreshes, and a few custom-coded sites where the client's needs warranted them. The engagement loop tightened during this stretch: discovery call → scoped quote → design → build → handoff with documentation.

2021

Adopted Webflow

Started building client sites in Webflow in 2021. The visual builder, real CMS, and clean code export made it the right fit for clients who needed both design polish and editable content — without the maintenance overhead of a hand-coded build. From this point on, most new clients had a "Webflow vs. WordPress" decision baked into the discovery call.

2021

Visual Design Direction (the Amaze Web brand)

Amaze Web sells design — so the brand and the agency's own site have to be the proof. Every visual decision had to read as the level of work clients would receive.

Show the work, not the wrapper

Client case studies lead. Marketing copy gets out of the way — the actual site screenshot is the pitch.

Plain pricing, plain process

Service tiers and a simple inquiry form. No quote dance — small businesses know within five seconds whether the budget fits.

Calm brand, strong client work

The agency layer stays neutral so client work — which ranges across visual styles — never fights with parent branding.

Buyer's path, not a dump

Case studies filter by platform (Webflow / Framer / WordPress / Custom) and industry. Prospects find a relevant example in two clicks, not twenty.

2020

Client Types

Most engagements fall into one of two patterns. The work is shaped to serve both well — without overbuilding for either.

SO

The Solo Operator

One-person business — coaching, freelance, creative

Goals

  • Look professional without an agency budget
  • Launch this month, not next quarter
  • Edit content themselves after handoff

Pain Points

  • Custom agencies cost five figures
  • Free templates look generic and unprofessional
IB

The Indie Brand

Small product brand needing storefront + email lift

Goals

  • Shopify storefront that converts
  • Brand-matched email design and flows
  • Open the door to AI assistant integrations

Pain Points

  • Subscription tools eat margin
  • Stitching together multiple platforms is brittle

2020

The Problem

Small businesses, indie brands, and creators wanted a real, polished web presence — but couldn't afford traditional agency rates and didn't trust generic free templates to represent them. Free options looked free. Agencies wanted five figures and three months. There was a gap in the middle: agency-quality work at fixed, transparent prices, with a clear engagement flow.

2020

First Client Site — Amaze Web Begins

The first paid client site shipped in 2020, built on a mix of Wix, HTML/CSS/JS, and WordPress depending on what fit the client. That first project became the template — same five-step engagement, same emphasis on letting the client edit their own content after launch — and Amaze Web has been taking clients through that same flow ever since.

Before 2020

Learning the Craft

The years before Amaze Web — high school and the first part of college — were spent learning to build websites end to end. Figma for design, HTML / CSS / JS for hand-coded sites, and WordPress for content-driven projects. Most of it was unpaid or for small projects close to home — but it built the foundation that made the first paid client site, and everything since, possible.

August 2024

Visual Design Direction

Artist portfolios live or die by how the work is framed. The design language was built to recede — typography, layout, and chrome had to all defer to the art on the wall.

Art on a wall, not a webpage

Generous whitespace, no boxes, no shadows. Each piece is presented like a hung work, not a tile in a grid.

3D as discovery

Three.js elements aren't decoration — they're navigation. Visitors move through the work rather than scrolling past it.

Restrained type

One serif for titles, one sans for utility. The artist's name is the loudest piece of type on any page; everything else recedes.

Dark mode primary

Default theme is dark to make pigments pop. Light mode exists for accessibility but the gallery was designed for the dark.

June 2024

Target Visitors

Two visitor patterns shaped the site — a collector who is evaluating the work, and a peer who arrived via social. The site had to serve both without picking a side.

CV

The Collector

Considering a piece for purchase or commission

Goals

  • See the body of work in context
  • Get specs, availability, price range
  • Reach the artist directly

Pain Points

  • Most artist sites lack basic specs
  • Inquiry forms feel like dead ends
PE

The Peer / Enthusiast

Found the artist on Instagram, wants the full picture

Goals

  • See more than the social grid shows
  • Read the artist's process or statement
  • Share specific pieces with friends

Pain Points

  • Social grids feel fragmented
  • Most artist sites haven't been updated
